**Notes on Revenue Administration & Land Records (Madras Presidency)**

**I. General Principles & Historical Context**

The system of land revenue administration in the Madras Presidency evolved over centuries, influenced by various rulers (Vijayanagara, Nayaks, Marathas, and the British).  Early systems were often based on assessments *in kind* (grain, produce) and labor services.  The British gradually transitioned towards a cash revenue system.  The core principle was maximizing revenue collection while maintaining a degree of agricultural stability.  

**II.  Land Tenure Systems**

Several land tenure systems existed, each with its own characteristics and implications for revenue collection:

*   **Zamindari System:**  Large estates held by Zamindars (landlords) who were responsible for collecting revenue from cultivators.  The Zamindars had hereditary rights, but were ultimately subject to British oversight.  This system was prevalent in many parts of the Presidency.
*   **Ryotwari System:**  Direct settlement with individual cultivators (ryots).  The ryot had ownership rights to the land, subject to payment of land revenue.  This system was more common in areas with smaller holdings and a more independent peasantry.
*   **Inam System:**  Grants of land made to individuals or institutions (temples, mosques, etc.) in recognition of services rendered.  These grants were often revenue-free or subject to nominal revenue payments.
*   **Kudivaram System:** A system where the land was held by tenants at will, with no fixed tenure or ownership rights.

**III. Revenue Assessment & Collection**

*   **Assessment:** Land revenue was assessed based on the soil quality, irrigation facilities, and the potential yield of the land.  Assessments were periodically revised (usually every 20-30 years) through a process called "settlement."
*   **Settlement Operations:**  Detailed surveys and investigations were conducted to determine the land's productivity and assess its revenue potential.  This involved examining village accounts, interviewing cultivators, and inspecting the land.
*   **Collection:** Revenue was collected through village headmen (Munsiffs) and other local officials.  The collected revenue was then remitted to the taluk (subdivision) and district treasury.
*   **Remission & Relief:**  Provisions existed for remitting revenue or granting relief to cultivators in times of famine, drought, or other natural calamities.  These provisions were often inadequate, leading to hardship and discontent.

**IV. Land Records & Documentation**

Maintaining accurate land records was crucial for revenue administration. Key documents included:

*   **Khasra:** A record of individual fields, their area, boundaries, and ownership.
*   **Jamabandi:** A record of the revenue assessment and collection for each village.
*   **Chitta:** A document showing the ownership and cultivation details of a particular plot of land.
*   **Adangal:** A register containing details of land revenue payments and arrears.
*   **Field Maps (Pattas):** Maps showing the boundaries of fields and their ownership.

**V.  Challenges & Reforms**

The revenue administration system faced several challenges:

*   **High Revenue Demand:**  Often, the revenue demand was excessive, leading to indebtedness and land alienation among cultivators.
*   **Corruption:**  Corruption among revenue officials was a persistent problem.
*   **Inaccurate Records:**  Land records were often outdated or inaccurate, leading to disputes and litigation.
*   **Rigidity:** The system was often inflexible and unable to respond effectively to changing economic conditions.

Reforms were attempted periodically to address these challenges, including:

*   **Ryotwari Reforms:**  Efforts to strengthen the rights of ryots and reduce the burden of revenue.
*   **Land Survey & Settlement:**  Regular land surveys and settlements to update land records and revise revenue assessments.
*   **Establishment of Revenue Courts:**  Creation of courts to resolve land disputes and address grievances.
